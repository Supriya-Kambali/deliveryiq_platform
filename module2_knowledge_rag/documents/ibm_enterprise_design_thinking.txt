WHAT IS ENTERPRISE DESIGN THINKING?
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Enterprise Design Thinking (EDT) is IBM's own methodology — an offshoot of
design thinking specifically designed for large, dispersed enterprise teams.
Standard design thinking breaks down at enterprise scale. EDT adds strategies,
tactics, and activities that uniquely scale design thinking across teams of
all shapes and sizes, whether co-located or widely dispersed.

Core mission: Give teams a shared language to align faster, focus on what
matters, and deliver with empathy and speed.

Core premise: We are not measured by the features and functions we ship.
We are measured by how well we fulfill our users' needs.

IBM is a pioneer in Enterprise Design Thinking. It has helped thousands of
teams across industries adopt a more aligned, human-centered approach to
operations and innovation.

THE THREE PRINCIPLES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

1. FOCUS ON USER OUTCOMES
Drive business by helping users achieve their goals. Shift the conversation
from features and functions to users and user outcomes. When teams focus on
user outcomes, they deliver more useful, usable, and desirable solutions.
They elevate professions and redefine industries.

Not every organization puts users first — some have explicit business rationale
(e.g., cost of delivery over UX in a commoditized industry). Design Thinking
requires acknowledging this tension honestly.

2. RESTLESS REINVENTION
Stay essential by treating everything as a prototype. Everything is a prototype.
Being essential — and staying essential — requires a continuous conversation
with users, responding to their changing needs through rapid prototyping and
constant iteration.

3. DIVERSE EMPOWERED TEAMS
Move faster by empowering diverse teams to act. Building diverse teams requires
actively seeking people with different perspectives across identity, experience,
and expertise. While it may seem counterintuitive, every dimension of diversity
is vital to a team's ability to manage complexity and generate breakthrough ideas.

THE LOOP — IBM'S CONTINUOUS LEARNING CYCLE
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

The Loop represents IBM's continuous cycle of understanding and delivering.
While you can start anywhere on the Loop, IBM recommends beginning with
Reflection to form intent.

OBSERVE — Immerse yourself in the real world
- Conduct user research in the actual context where users work
- Do not rely on assumptions — we are not our users
- Design research leads teams to continuously build understanding and empathy
- Methods: contextual inquiry, user interviews, field observation, journey mapping

REFLECT — Come together and look within
- Synthesize observations as a team
- Share what you saw, heard, and felt
- Identify patterns, tensions, and opportunities
- Align on the most critical problems to solve

MAKE — Give concrete form to abstract ideas
- Build prototypes — physical, digital, or conceptual
- Prototypes don't have to be working software
- Make ideas tangible so you can get feedback from real users
- The act of making reveals what you don't yet understand

THE THREE KEYS — SCALING DESIGN THINKING TO ENTERPRISE TEAMS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Complex problems require complex teams. Project management frameworks help
manage that complexity. IBM may divide teams into "squads" or "workstreams,"
or divide time into "sprints" or "phases." The three Keys align teams across
both space and time.

KEY 1: HILLS — Align teams on meaningful user outcomes

Hills are statements of intent written as meaningful user outcomes.
They tell you WHERE to go, not HOW to get there. This empowers teams to explore
breakthrough ideas without losing sight of the goal.

A Hill is NOT a feature list. A Hill is a user outcome statement.
Example of a weak Hill: "Build a cloud migration portal"
Example of a strong Hill: "A mid-sized bank's IT team can migrate 80% of their
on-premise workloads to IBM Cloud without engaging external consultants"

Hills define success based on discrete user outcomes. They turn human needs into
project goals by aligning the whole team around a common understanding of success,
without prescribing tactics that sub-teams should figure out independently.

When you use Enterprise Design Thinking, you use Hills to define intended user
outcomes AND the breakdown of your teams. For each Hill team, assign a core
leadership team with functional leads from each discipline. Grant them authority
for day-to-day triage and hold them accountable for achieving their outcome.

KEY 2: PLAYBACKS — Stay aligned by regularly exchanging feedback

Playbacks are regular check-ins that bring users, stakeholders, and teams
together to tell stories and exchange feedback. They reveal misalignment and
measure progress against the big-picture problem being solved.

Playbacks capture the nuances of users' context by telling stories from their
perspective — not from the perspective of the delivery team's internal progress.
They are a "safe space" to surface misalignment before it becomes a blocker.

Cadence: Playbacks should happen regularly — aligned to sprint cycles. Not just
at project milestones.

KEY 3: SPONSOR USERS — Invite users into the work

Sponsor Users are real-world users that regularly contribute their domain
expertise to your team. They help teams stay in touch with real users' real-world
needs throughout the project — not just at requirements gathering.

Sponsor Users are crucial for keeping the team grounded in reality. They are
partners in design, not just subjects of research.

MEASURING SUCCESS IN ENTERPRISE DESIGN THINKING
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

You are what you measure. Paying attention only to revenue and operating costs
undermines the team's effort to focus on problems that matter most to users.

IBM recommends choosing appropriate USER OUTCOME METRICS:
- Usability: Can users accomplish their goals efficiently?
- Usefulness: Does the solution solve the real problem?
- Desirability: Do users want to use this solution?

Measure these both in development and in-market. Not just at launch.

THE IBM DESIGN THINKING TOOLKIT — PRACTICAL ACTIVITIES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

IBM provides a public toolkit of guided activities. Key ones for delivery
consultants:

- HILLS WORKSHOP: Clearly state intent in terms of user and market value.
  Use when: team needs alignment on what success looks like.

- STAKEHOLDER MAP: Identify project stakeholders, their expectations, and
  their relationships to each other.
  Use when: starting a new engagement or when stakeholder dynamics are unclear.

- AS-IS SCENARIO MAP: Build a better understanding of users' current experience.
  Use when: the team lacks empathy for what users go through today.

- EMPATHY MAP: Build empathy for users through a conversation informed by
  team observations.
  Use when: team is designing for users they haven't spent time with.

- ASSUMPTIONS MAP: When the team needs a "reality check," identify assumptions
  and the best ways to address them.
  Use when: the project is moving fast and the team hasn't validated key beliefs.

- PRIORITIZATION GRID: Decide the next move by focusing on the intersection
  of importance and feasibility.
  Use when: the team has many options and needs a structured way to choose.

- AI ETHICS EXPLORATION: Determine why you would or wouldn't use AI to solve
  users' problems. Brainstorm AI's impact on people and the world.
  Use when: considering AI as part of the solution.

CONSULTANT QUICK REFERENCE — COMMON EDT QUESTIONS
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Q: Client says they already did design thinking workshops. What do we add?
A: Standard Design Thinking is for small teams. IBM EDT adds the Keys (Hills,
Playbacks, Sponsor Users) specifically to scale across large enterprise teams
that are geographically distributed and cross-functional. Ask if they had real
Sponsor Users involved throughout, not just at kickoff.

Q: How do we run a Hills workshop in a 2-hour session?
A: 1. Set context: share user research findings (15 min)
   2. Individually write candidate Hills (20 min)
   3. Share and cluster similar Hills (15 min)
   4. Vote on the most impactful (10 min)
   5. Refine top 3 Hills as a group (30 min)
   6. Test each Hill: Is it a user outcome or a feature? Rewrite if it's a feature.

Q: A client PM is resistant to Playbacks. How to convince them?
A: Playbacks are not status meetings — they are misalignment detectors. Every
week teams spend building in the wrong direction because stakeholders weren't
aligned costs 5x more than the Playback itself. Frame it as risk reduction, not
an extra ceremony.

Q: How does EDT apply to AI projects?
A: IBM has specific EDT toolkit activities for AI: identifying data sources,
ethical impact brainstorming, and AI assumption mapping. The Hill for an AI
project should still be a user outcome — not "build an AI model" but "a claims
processor can resolve 90% of routine claims without human escalation."
